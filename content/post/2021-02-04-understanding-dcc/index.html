Welcome!

Recently, I’ve been running a game of Dungeon Crawl Classics (DCC) 1 for my regular RPG group, and I’ve been having a blast. Not surprisingly, this enthusiasm has come with a desire to do a bit of tweaking to the game, making it into what I call a “House Game”—that is, a set of rules tailored to exactly how you like to run an RPG. I believe that most people who run RPGs 2 have this to some degree. Sometimes, making a House Game involves making a whole new RPG, but more often it’s just a few tweaks and houserules to a beloved base system to make it your own.

This blog, Jeu de la Hache 3, is meant to record my process at turning DCC into a House Game for use at my tables. I’m mostly writing this for myself, since giving air to a largely internal design process has always helped me make better designs. However, I also hope that this blog can serve as an introduction to DCC and an invitation to other RPG players and designers to give this very excellent RPG a look.

Why DCC?

Like virtually every other long-time RPG player out there, I’ve played a lot of fantasy dungeon-crawling adventure. In part, because it’s what’s popular and so it’s what’s easy to build a group around, but in part because it’s a fun genre that I and many others enjoy. Like its more popular cousin D&D, DCC is a game of itinerant adventurers causing trouble, solving said trouble, and busting into tombs and making off with the loot therein. This makes it useful as a House Game, since it’s a popular and well-understood by most RPG players. In fact, the core mechanics at play in DCC should be familiar to most:

  1. The Judge describes a scenario to the player(s).
  2. The player describes how their character(s) act and interact in that scenario.
  3. If their character attempts an action that has an unknown and potentially interesting outcome, the Judge will ask for a roll.
    1. This is usually a d20 roll.
    2. Add a relevant ability score modifier, plus other situational bonuses or penalites as applicable.
    3. Higher rolls are usually better.
    4. You succeed if you meet or beat a set difficulty number.
  4. The Judge narrates the outcome of your action (be it something simple like “you climb the rope” or more mechanically complex like “you hit the monster; roll weapon damage)”.

If that sequence of events is familiar to you, you can probably sit down at a game of DCC, pick up a character sheet, and jump into the game, with the extra rules and changes explained to you as they become relevant.

So, if what I’m looking for in a House Game is a fantasy dungeon-romper, why go with DCC in particular? This is a rich RPG space, filled with such behemoths as D&D 5e, Pathfinder, and the collected works of the OSR, so what makes DCC stand out to me, and why do I think you should take a look at it too? There’s a few things that I love about DCC, and the best way I know to illustrate them is to show how they are similar to and different from these other games. Please note that none of the following is meant to be a condemnation of any of these other games: I’m a fan of all of them 4, and they do what they do well. DCC exists in the same neighborhood as these games, so it is useful to understand it in relation to them, and in a certain sense I think it is a better game for my purposes than any of the others, but that is not to say it’s a better game per se or that you should feel bad if you like one of these other games more.

The Complex: D&D and Pathfinder

One of the great strengths of D&D 5e and Pathfinder is the mechanical breadth of character options. When you make a Fighter, it feels really different from making a Wizard or a Rogue. Every level, you get new powers and abilities that say “This is my character, this is what they can do, this is what makes them unique at the table and the world.” To me, a major part of the fun in an RPG comes from making meaningful choices, and having the class you pick to play be meaningfully distinct from the other classes you could have picked is a major example of this.

Although DCC only has seven classes, they are each designed to play very differently from each other, perhaps even moreso than D&D. Warriors aren’t just “good at fighting;” a DCC Warrior gets an ability called a Mighty Deed of Arms that means 1/3 of the time they hit something (at first level), they can do a near-supernatural feat of prowess in addition to the attack, from a disarm or grapple to a skull-crushing blow that temporarily blinds their opponent or an attack that hews their foe in twain and sends their corpse crashing into their comrades, knocking them over. All of the DCC classes have something like this—a class-specific ability that lets them do something no-one else can.

Similarly, the magic system in DCC is mechanically rich, with each spell feeling like a unique piece of magic. Like D&D, a Wizard (or Cleric, though they are a bit different) knows a specific number of spells taken from a rich list provided by the game rules. However, Wizards are not limited in how many times they may attempt a spell. Instead, they roll a spell check (much like how a Warrior must roll an attack roll to hit). The effect of the spell check determines not only whether the spell succeeds or not, but also the spell’s effects, with higher rolls giving more powerful versions of the spell.

All this is to say that DCC has a mechanical richness/complexity closer to D&D/Pathfinder than it does to the OSR games, which aligns with my and my table’s desires in an RPG. However, it has a sharp difference between these other complex games in terms of the philosophy of character design. DCC leans heavily on randomness in its character creation: in fact, in the base rules, you “make a character” by rolling up four peasants completely randomly and then picking a class for any that survive their first adventure.

This heavy reliance on randomness means it is almost impossible to have a specific “build” for a character. This has been my greatest complaint with D&D 5e: the combat focus and explosion of character options means there is an increasing tendency to make characters completely designed around a specific set of rules interactions, which feels like a competition between players trying to make characters that are absurdly good at shutting down specific kinds of challenges and Judges that need to stay on top of all the rules interactions to make appropriate challenges for them. DCC does away with this problem completely by having many details of your character left up to chance, and then invites you to “quest for it” if you want to make specific changes or gain new powers, rather than selecting them from a list at level-up.

The Simple: OSR

On the other end of the spectrum lie the OSR (Old-School Revival/Renaissance) games. These are games that try to get back to the roots of the RPG hobby, emulating or re-organizing the original D&D rules as designed by Gygax and Arneson. Most of what DCC does differently from these games is already covered in the above (i.e. it is more mechanically complex and has more richness in how classes and spells differ from each other). Here, I will instead focus on the similarities between DCC and the OSR, which helps to enrich the DCC experience overall.

The whole motivation behind DCC is to get back to “Appendix N” 5 fantasy gaming, and so in that sense, DCC is part of the OSR. Its mechanics look quite different from most other OSR games, and so people think of it as OSR-adjacent rather than a “true OSR” game, but in terms of philosophy, this game is 100% meant to evoke feelings of the original RPGs from the 70s. Indeed, DCC’s reliance on very weird dice (the D7 is a favourite of mine) is an intentional decision to evoke the feeling of the first time you picked up a D20 and asked what on earth it was.

A big part of the OSR games is a sort of DIY spirit. Rather than try to have a specific rule defined for every situation a character may encouter, OSR games empower Judges to make up an on-the-fly ruling based on common sense and table buy-in. This “OSR spirit” is alive and well in DCC. I mentioned this briefly above, but a major DCC principle is the “quest for it” mentality. If a player wants a specific character ability or magic item, they are encouraged to spend some time in-game researching and then questing for it. For example, base DCC doesn’t have a barbarian class, and there are no rules for Warriors gaining rage-based combat abilities. If a player wanted to play such a berserker character, the DCC solution would be to seek out the lost caste of rage-trance warriors buried beneath the Ironfang mountains and convince them to share their techniques. In a more general sense, DCC’s OSR philosophy strongly encourages character development and mechanical advancement to happen at the table, in the game, as part of the story.

The other good OSR-like aspect of DCC comes in the combat rules. Specifically, most characters (including monsters and NPCs) have very few hitpoints compared to D&D 5e. This ensures that combat is fast and brutal, and means that a single solid roll from an attacker can drop almost any foe, making fights uncertain and tense. DCC has a reputation as a very lethal game, and honestly, this is talked up a little bit more than is true; after your peasants become true characters, they are hardy enough for a few tumbles in the adventure. However, unlike our D&D days, my DCC table has seen a character near death almost every fight, meaning fights are not entered lightly and often more memorable when they do happen.

Conclusion

To summarize, I’m focusing my homebrewing efforts on DCC because it is a fantasy dungeon crawler that occupies a sweet spot for me in terms of having enough mechanical complexity to make character-based choices feel meaningful and distinct, without so much that a large part of the game becomes about making optimized character builds away from the table. Instead, the focus is on developing interesting characters based on their adventures and mishaps at the table. It’s fun, it’s fast, and it’s straightforward without being same-y after a few levels. Combat is exciting and lethal, magic is chaotic and magical, but overall the rules are familiar enough that it’s a pretty easy pitch to convince people who have only ever played a single RPG to learn them.

I focused on character rules in this exploration of DCC, in part because I’ve found that the rules governing characters are the most important for setting the tone of a game, and in part because DCC is pretty sparse on rules for Judges. This is going to be a major focus for some of the homebrewing that is coming in the next few posts: setting up some rule systems to help make other, non-character based parts of the game feel more mechanically driven and less Judge fiat-based.
