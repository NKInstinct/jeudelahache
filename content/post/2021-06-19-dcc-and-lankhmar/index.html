What’s a Lankhmar?

DCC Lankhmar is a boxed set of DCC rules and adventures set in the world of Nehwon, from Fritz Lieber’s Fafhard and the Grey Mouser stories 1. Lankhmar, the largest city in Nehwon, is a foetid pit of skulduggery and an excellent location for the kinds of morally grey stories DCC likes to tell. As interesting as the setting is, however, it’s not what I’m looking for in a House Game—not that I don’t like Lankhmar, I’m just looking for something a little more generic so that I can more readily adapt a given game to the players.

Rules-wise, DCC Lankhmar introduces a couple of very interesting new mechanics to a DCC game. Goodman Games has said that there will never be a “2nd edition” of DCC that invalidates the previous editions, but the general sentiment around Lankhmar is that the new rules here are the next closest thing. There are three rules from Lankhmar that I will be lifting into my House Game version of DCC, as detailed below.

No Funnel

Adventurers in Lankhmar are already heroes, and so newly made characters start at level 1 with a class already, and a couple changes to how stats are rolled to “simulate” the attrition of bad characters in the funnel. As fun as a level 0 funnel is to run, it’s a bit more work to set up for each new group and in my experience, makes selling the idea of DCC in general a bit more difficult, so I’m going with the Lankhmar version of character creation.

Benesons and Dooms

Lankhmar characters each get a unique beneson (a random special power based on where they are from) and doom (similar, but a drawback), and then an option to buy a second one by spending luck based on how good it is. This has been very well-received in my group, and helps to bring back a little bit of the “build a character concept” feeling that people used to D&D miss when they come to DCC, without pitching the game into build-land.

Fleeting Luck

In addition to their Luck stat, which works as normal, Lankhmar heroes have a Fleeting Luck resource that lets them be a little faster and looser with spending luck to improve results in their favour. My games are all about finding ways to encourage players to take creative control of the narrative, so an extra way for them to make a situation go in a way they are excited about is good in my books.

Tweaks to base DCC

Having taken those three rules from Lankhmar, I’m going to also make a couple of changes to the base DCC classes to better fit my vision for a House Game. In particular, I’m going to remove the race-as-class element of the DCC classes by re-flavouring them into more generic character archetypes. Race-as-class helps to anchor DCC in Appendix N-style fantasy, but it isn’t what I’m going for with my game.

Dwarf becomes Marauder

The DCC Dwarf is basically a Warrior with some slightly tuned-down abilities (fewer HP, critical tables don’t go as high) in exchange for a unique shield-bash attack and a few dwarf-y abilities. To me, this conjures up an image of a somewhat gruff highwayman-type character, specializing sword-and-board fighting and obtaining money. The specific reflavourings and changes I will make are:

Infravision becomes Night-Eye: Your years of nighttime raids and life in the wild have left you accustomed to operating in the dark. You can “see” in extremely low-light environments out to 60’, though you can’t make out fine details of things you are seeing.

Slow gets removed. 2

Underground Training becomes Knave-sense: You’ve seen every dirty trick in the book and know how to avoid them. Add your class level to any checks to detect traps or tricks in your surrounding environment. Additionally, you have an excellent sense of where loot might be found, and can sense the direction of large piles of gold and gems within 100’. Studying a mark or a location for a moment can let you detect even a single coin.

Elf becomes Spellsword

The DCC Elf is already a cross between a Wizard and a Warrior, so most class features only need a slight tweak:

Magic: The spellsword must perfect both martial and magical training; the indwelling of these two opposed forces cause a number of strange effects to manifest in their being.

  • The close proximity of magic and metal in a spellsword’s life sets up an antipathic effect, where prolonged contact with iron and other base metals causes a painful burning sensation. The Spellsword cannot wear armour or wield weapons made of metals except for mithril. Because of this, spellswords may start the game with a mithril weapon and set of armour bought for the same price as a normal variant.
  • The spellsword has learned to channel small amounts of arcane power to enhance their vigilance. The spellsword can see in the dark out to 60’, gains a +4 bonus to detect secret doors, and cannot be magically paralyzed or forced to sleep
  • Learning both arms and magic is taxing, and the spellsword enlists help whenever possible. They receive patron bond and invoke patron spells at 1st level automatically.
  • Finally, the spellsword’s magical training tends to focus more on mastering a single “signature” spell than the more well-rounded Wizard. The spellsword may choose to apply their luck bonus at 1st level to a single spell’s spellcheck. This bonus does not change, even if the spellsword’s luck modifier changes later.

Halfling becomes Noble

This might be the biggest stretch of the three, but since the halfling is largely about luck and sharing luck around the team, the class speaks to me of a team coordinator. Most of the rules are unchanged, just the reasons behind them become a bit different:

Two-Weapon Fighting: You are trained in the art of fencing, and can fight comfortably with a weapon in either hand. This is fashionably a rapier and main gauche, but you are not limited to this selection.

Small Size is removed.

Slow is removed.

Infravision is removed.

Stealth: A combination of practice at intrigue and a keen desire to save your own skin have made you particularly good at vanishing when needed.

Good Luck Charm becomes Blue-Blooded: Fortune has always smiled on your family, and luck seems always on your side. This can even extend to your comrades, who can benefit from your luck if you are able to see them and give a bit of encouragement, insight, or warning.

What about races

Now that we’ve removed race-as-class, we can turn to Benesons and Dooms to put races back in the game. Race as a concept in RPGs is somewhat problematic; the better word would probably be species, but that gives a more modern/sci-fi vibe so feels out of place in a fantasy game. Lankhmar avoids this problem by only having humans, and then providing different backgrounds through the different benesons that they can possess.

Our game will do something similar: the Lankhmar-specific names of the backgrounds will be changed to something more generic, and will also make space for players to play a specific fantasy species if they want but not be limited to it. The other nice thing about benesons rather than racial abilities from other games is that there are 20 to chose from for each background and you choose them randomly. So you don’t have the problem where suddenly all elves are good at longbows and forestry. Instead, there’s a chance that if you are playing an elf, they might have notable longbow or forestry skills, but probably not both, and they’re just as likely to be noteworthy for a specific political connection. This goes a long way to make “elf” feel more like a culture and less like a stereotype.

Backgrounds/Heritages

Lankhmar becomes Metropolis (human)

Land of the Eight Cities becomes Woodland Realm (Elf)

Cold Waste becomes Mountains (Dwarf)

Eastern Lands becomes Estates (Halfling)

Mingol Steppes becomes Badlands (Orc)

Under this new conception of backgrounds, the species indicated in parantheses are just there to help ground the choice in common parlance. If a player wants to play a “typical” orc, they could pick Badlands and roll, but there’s nothing saying they couldn’t also decide to play a dwarf who was raised in the badlands and who learned to ride a mule to great effect.

Avoiding non-heroes

The final new rule I’m going to add to character creation is a reroll qualifier like what was present in 3rd edition D&D. Players will roll their stats as normal for a Lankhmar character, and then when they are done, if the sum of their modifiers is zero or less, they may erase all six of their attributes and roll them again. While playing a character with terrible stats could be a fun challenge for some players (and indeed, the choice to reroll is always a choice), this rule provides an unbiased way to rescue a player from a character they aren’t ever going to be excited about.

Conclusion

Combining in these new rules introduced in Lankhmar helps to broaden the character choices made a bit during character creation, which should help newcomers to DCC feel a bit more in control of what their experience will be like. This also has the benefit of addressing race/heritage in fantasy in a way that avoids blanket stereotypes (no more “All orcs are strong and dumb, all gnomes learn to speak with animals”) but also avoids going too far the other way and pretending that all heritages are identical.
